## Data types

COLMAP's `automatic_reconstructor` accepts three data types that affect how images are matched.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="INDIVIDUAL">
    Unordered photos taken from arbitrary viewpoints with no assumed temporal relationship.

    - Best for photo collections, tourist photos, and any set of images where capture order is irrelevant
    - Uses vocabulary tree or exhaustive matching to find overlapping image pairs

    ```bash
    colmap automatic_reconstructor \
        --workspace_path /path/to/workspace \
        --image_path /path/to/images \
        --data_type INDIVIDUAL
    ```
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="VIDEO">
    Sequential frames extracted from a video, where adjacent frames have high overlap.

    - Uses sequential matching, which only compares nearby frames in the sequence
    - Much faster than exhaustive matching for large frame counts
    - Assumes consistent frame order in the image directory

    ```bash
    colmap automatic_reconstructor \
        --workspace_path /path/to/workspace \
        --image_path /path/to/frames \
        --data_type VIDEO
    ```
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="INTERNET">
    Crowd-sourced images downloaded from the internet, such as images of a landmark from many different photographers.

    - High variability in viewpoints, illumination, and image quality
    - Uses vocabulary tree matching to efficiently find overlapping pairs in large collections

    ```bash
    colmap automatic_reconstructor \
        --workspace_path /path/to/workspace \
        --image_path /path/to/images \
        --data_type INTERNET
    ```
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Capture guidelines

The quality of the reconstruction depends heavily on the quality of the input images and how they are captured. Follow these guidelines for best results.

### Scene texture

<Warning>
COLMAP's feature matching relies on visual texture. Textureless surfaces (white walls, smooth floors, sky) produce very few keypoints and will be poorly reconstructed or not reconstructed at all.
</Warning>

Choose scenes with rich surface texture — brick, stone, patterned surfaces, or any surface with distinctive visual features.

### Illumination

Capture images under **similar illumination conditions**:

- Avoid mixing images taken at different times of day or in different weather
- Avoid images where the scene is partially in shadow and partially in bright sunlight
- Consistent lighting makes feature matching more reliable

### Visual overlap

Ensure **high visual overlap** between images:

- Every part of the scene should appear in **at least 3 images** for reliable triangulation
- For buildings or objects, move in small steps and maintain significant overlap (50–80%) between adjacent shots
- Isolated images with little or no overlap to the rest of the collection cannot be registered

### Viewpoints

Capture the scene from **different viewpoints** to enable triangulation:

- Purely planar image sets (all images taken from the same height and direction) can lead to degenerate reconstructions
- Include images from multiple heights, angles, and distances around the subject
- For buildings, photograph corners and sides from different approach angles

### Video frame rate

When using video as input:

- Avoid extracting every single frame from a high-frame-rate video — adjacent frames may be too similar, creating redundant data and slowing matching
- Extract frames at a rate that maintains moderate overlap between consecutive frames (e.g., every 5th–10th frame for typical walking speeds)
- Too few frames risks gaps in coverage; too many frames creates unnecessary redundancy

<Tip>
For handheld video capture, moving slowly and steadily produces better frame overlap than rapid motion. Avoid motion blur — blur degrades feature detection.
</Tip>
